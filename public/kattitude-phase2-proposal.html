01 · The Foundation

Every booking needs somewhere to land.

The AI booking system is a client-facing flow. When a client submits a request, uploads reference photos, receives an estimate, signs a waiver, and pays a deposit — all of that data writes somewhere. That somewhere is the Phase 1 dashboard. This is a technical dependency — the sequencing is structural.

Phase 2 connects to infrastructure that Phase 1 builds and runs. Every booking that flows through the system writes to four places simultaneously — the appointment calendar, the deposit ledger, the client record, and the artist's schedule. Phase 1 builds all four. Phase 2 uses all four.

What Phase 2 writes to
Where do bookings go?
The appointment system — tracking confirmed sessions, start times, and assigned artist.
Connects to Dashboard · Appointments (Phase 1)
Where are deposits tracked?
The deposit ledger — showing paid, outstanding, and total revenue by artist and date.
Connects to Dashboard · Deposits (Phase 1)
How does the artist see their schedule?
The artist portal — their upcoming sessions, client reference photos, and session notes.
Connects to Dashboard · Artist Portal (Phase 1)
Where does client info live?
The client record — contact info, session history, signed waivers, and uploaded reference photos.
Connects to Dashboard · Clients (Phase 1)
What happens if Phase 2 comes first
Building Phase 2 standalone means building appointment management, deposit tracking, client records, and artist scheduling inside the booking system itself — the same four systems Phase 1 already builds. The result is duplicate infrastructure, a longer timeline, and a higher cost. Phase 1 first means Phase 2 plugs into a foundation that already exists and already works. The build is faster, the cost is lower, and the result is a single connected system rather than two parallel ones.

02 · What Phase 2 Builds

A booking that runs before you lift a finger.

A client discovers Kattitude, picks up their phone, and books — reference photos uploaded, estimate received, waiver signed, deposit paid. By the time they walk through the door, their appointment is confirmed, their artist knows what they're getting, and everything is already in the dashboard.

The Full Booking Flow
01
Style & Placement Client selects their tattoo style (traditional, realism, blackwork, watercolor, etc.) and body placement from a guided selection.
02
Reference Photo Upload Client uploads 1–5 reference images. Photos attach to their client record in the dashboard and are visible to the assigned artist before the session.
03
Estimate Generation The AI analyzes complexity, size, placement, and style and generates a time and cost range. The assigned artist reviews and confirms before it reaches the client.
04
Artist Selection & Scheduling Client picks a preferred artist or "first available." Real availability is drawn from each artist's schedule in the Phase 1 dashboard — only open slots are shown.
05
Digital Waiver Client signs the studio waiver digitally before confirming. Stored in their Phase 1 client record with name, email, and timestamp.
06
Deposit Payment Client pays a deposit via Stripe to confirm the appointment. Payment records directly in the Phase 1 deposit tracker.
07
Confirmation & Dashboard Update Booking confirmed. Appointment appears in the dashboard, artist schedule updates, client record is created or updated, deposit is logged — all simultaneously, all automatically.

03 · The Estimation Engine

How a reference photo becomes a quote.

The estimate step starts with the artist. Over time, as session data flows through the Phase 1 dashboard — actual time vs. estimated time, complexity scores, each artist's pace — the AI layer calibrates. Quotes get more accurate with every session that runs through the system.

AI Image Analysis
  • Reference photo processing — the AI analyzes each uploaded reference image for complexity (linework density, shading depth, color count), estimates surface area based on placement selection, and classifies the style (traditional, realism, blackwork, watercolor, etc.).
  • Complexity scoring — each piece receives a 1–10 complexity score that maps to a time estimate. Fine-line portrait realism scores higher than a simple flash piece. The score is visible to the client, making estimates transparent rather than opaque.
Artist Performance Profiles
  • Speed profiles built from Phase 1 data — the Phase 1 clock-in/clock-out system tracks actual session duration for every appointment. Each artist's average time per complexity point is derived from real sessions — grounded in actual data. Artist A might average 45 minutes per point, Artist B 30 minutes. Quotes adjust per artist accordingly.
  • Precedent-based pricing — as more sessions complete and log actual vs. estimated times, the AI calibrates to your studio's real history. A quote for a medium-complexity blackwork sleeve in six months is grounded in dozens of similar sessions your artists have already completed.
Availability Engine
  • Real schedule integration — the scheduler reads from the Phase 1 artist portal: existing bookings, working hours, estimated session duration based on complexity score and artist speed profile, and a configurable buffer between clients (default 30 minutes for cleanup and reset).
  • Live slot generation — clients see only genuinely available appointment slots. As bookings confirm and schedules fill, the available times update in real time. Every slot shown is confirmed open.
How the AI Gets Better Over Time
Phase 2 launches with artist-reviewed estimates — the AI suggests, the artist confirms. Every confirmed session creates a data point: complexity score, estimated time, actual time, artist. After 30–50 sessions, the AI's estimates become reliable enough to send directly to clients for review, with the artist approving rather than generating. The system earns its autonomy through your studio's real data.
